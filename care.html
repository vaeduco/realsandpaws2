Care Tips 🦴

Friendly, practical basics to help your best friend stay happy, healthy, and well-loved. Tap a topic below to dig into the details.

  • Feed a complete, age-appropriate diet (puppy, adult, or senior) and follow the portion guidance on the label, adjusting for your dog's weight and activity.
  • Stick to a consistent schedule — most adult dogs do well with two measured meals a day rather than free-feeding.
  • Always keep fresh, clean water available and refill it daily.
  • Keep treats to about 10% of daily calories so meals stay balanced.
  • Avoid foods that are toxic to dogs, including chocolate, grapes and raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol (a sweetener), macadamia nuts, and alcohol.
  • Make any diet change gradually over 7–10 days to avoid an upset stomach.

  • Brush regularly — short coats may need it weekly, while long or double coats benefit from a few sessions each week to prevent mats.
  • Bathe only as needed (often every few weeks to a few months) using a dog-formulated shampoo; over-bathing can dry out skin.
  • Trim nails when you can hear them clicking on the floor, taking care to avoid the sensitive quick.
  • Check and gently clean ears, and watch for redness, odor, or excess wax that could signal a problem.
  • Brush teeth several times a week with dog-safe toothpaste — never human toothpaste — to support dental health.
  • Use grooming time to check for lumps, ticks, fleas, or skin irritation, and make it a calm, positive experience.

  • Aim for daily activity, tailored to breed, age, and health — many dogs thrive on roughly 30 minutes to two hours of movement a day.
  • Mix it up with walks, fetch, and sniff-friendly exploring; dogs love using their noses.
  • Add mental enrichment like puzzle toys, training games, and chew toys to prevent boredom.
  • Be mindful in hot weather: walk during cooler hours, bring water, and watch for overheating; avoid hot pavement that can burn paws.
  • Ease puppies and senior dogs into exercise gently, and avoid high-impact activity on growing joints.
  • Watch for signs of overexertion such as heavy panting, lagging behind, or limping, and rest when needed.

  • Schedule regular veterinary checkups and keep vaccinations and parasite prevention (fleas, ticks, heartworm) up to date.
  • Talk with your vet about spaying or neutering and about microchipping for safe identification.
  • Keep your dog at a healthy weight — you should be able to feel the ribs without pressing hard — to protect joints and organs.
  • Watch for warning signs like changes in appetite, energy, drinking, bathroom habits, or unexplained lumps, and call your vet if they persist.
  • Know basic emergency signs (difficulty breathing, repeated vomiting, collapse, suspected poisoning) and have your vet and a poison-control number handy.
  • Don't forget emotional health: companionship, routine, gentle training, and play all keep your dog content.
🩺

These tips are friendly general guidance, not medical advice. Every dog is unique, so please consult a licensed veterinarian for advice tailored to your pup's age, breed, and health — especially if something seems off.
